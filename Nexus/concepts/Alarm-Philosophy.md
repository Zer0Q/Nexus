# Alarm Philosophy

## Definition
A documented framework that defines alarm objectives, prioritization schemes, classification rules, design principles, and lifecycle management requirements. Serves as the guiding reference for all alarm-related decisions, performance benchmarks, and operator expectations.

## Why It Matters
Without a philosophy, alarm decisions are ad hoc and inconsistent. The philosophy establishes clear expectations about alarm behavior, operator response, and system performance — it is the foundation that rationalization, design, and monitoring all reference.

## Key Ideas
- **Required by ISA-18.2:** must define alarm classes, prioritization, classification, response expectations
- **Contents:** alarm objectives, scope, roles/responsibilities, prioritization scheme, classification rules, design principles, performance metrics, training requirements
- **Living document:** must be updated as practices evolve — philosophy out of sync with practice will be abandoned
- **Audit target:** formal audits verify that work processes align with the philosophy
- **ISA-TR18.2.1** provides detailed guidance on developing a structured alarm philosophy
- **Template approach:** one philosophy for multiple sites with local modifications (e.g., ACME Chemicals: template for 3 sites)

## Tradeoffs
- Too vague = generic decisions; too specific = brittle to change
- Requires cross-functional input: operations, maintenance, engineering, safety
- Maintenance burden: must be reviewed and updated regularly
