# Outcome Driven Research

## Definition
Choosing an outcome you genuinely want to exist and reasoning backwards to the experiments needed to achieve it, rather than reading the literature and hunting for incremental improvements. Manufactures originality by dragging you into territory no survey paper covers.

## Why It Matters
The trouble with an absorbed problem is that you hold the conclusion without the reasoning. You know some famous lab cares about a direction, but not why, not what they expect to find, not what would make them drop it. When they pivot, you find out a year later. On a fashionable problem, you are racing a thousand people who started earlier and have more compute.

## Key Ideas
- John Schulman's guide to ML research splits work into two modes: literature-driven (read papers, hunt for improvements) vs outcome-driven (choose a goal, reason backwards)
- A goal you actually care about will drag you into territory no survey paper covers
- Originality comes from wanting something specific, not from finding a gap in the literature
- Combines with taste: predict outcomes, choose the ones that matter, work backwards from there

## Tradeoffs
- Outcome-driven research can lead you down paths with no existing literature to guide you
- Requires the taste to choose outcomes that are both important and achievable
- May produce negative results — but negative results with clear reasoning are still contributions
