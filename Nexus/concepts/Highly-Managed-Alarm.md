# Highly Managed Alarm

## Definition
An alarm class requiring more administration and documentation than standard alarms. HMAs cover alarms critical to process safety, personnel protection, environmental compliance, product quality, and commercial loss. Criteria for HMA assignment must be defined in the alarm philosophy.

## Why It Matters
HMAs represent the alarms where failure has the highest consequences. Extra management rigor (testing, training, auditing, documentation) ensures these alarms remain reliable and effective throughout their lifecycle.

## Key Ideas
- **HMA criteria:** process safety (human life), personnel protection, environmental protection, CGMP compliance, commercial loss, product quality, licensor requirements, company policy
- **Extra requirements:** more frequent testing, specialized training, detailed documentation, stricter MOC, dedicated audit schedules
- **IPL alarms:** Independent Protection Layer alarms are a key HMA type — require specific monitoring (frequency, time in alarm, time shelved, time out of service, flood participation)
- **H2S toxic gas example:** managed H2S class alarm caught a setpoint change that would have masked a safety concern; unmanaged, the change would have gone unnoticed
- **Not all alarms are HMAs:** right-size by splitting out key HMAs and leaving the rest as general class
- **Prevents erosion:** HMA classification helps maintain alarm integrity as processes change over time

## Tradeoffs
- More paperwork and complexity — justified by the consequences of failure
- Requires dedicated resources for ongoing management and auditing
