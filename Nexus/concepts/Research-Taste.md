# Research Taste

## Definition
The ability to predict experiment outcomes, identify important unsolved problems, and forecast which research directions will matter in two years. Not an innate gift but a trainable muscle built through repeated prediction-plus-correction cycles.

## Why It Matters
Most researchers absorb problems from advisors, big labs, or trending papers — holding conclusions without the reasoning. Taste manufactured through deliberate practice leads you into territory no survey paper covers, because you chose the outcome you genuinely want to exist.

## Key Ideas
- Training method: predict the result of every experiment before running it, cover a paper's results and guess numbers from the method alone, forecast which releases will matter in two years and check hit rate later
- A forecast plus a correction, repeated a few hundred times, is how every good model gets trained — including the one in your head
- Richard Hamming's habit: ask colleagues what the important problems in their field are and why they aren't working on them
- John Schulman's outcome-driven mode: choose an outcome you genuinely want to exist and reason backwards to experiments — manufactures originality
- Shared reading lists produce shared ideas — if your information diet is trending arXiv plus group chat filter, you reach the same conclusions as everyone else at the same time

## Tradeoffs
- Taste takes hundreds of prediction cycles to develop — not useful in the short term
- Cross-disciplinary range matters as much as depth: interpretability borrows from neuroscience, eval design is mechanism design
- Old material is criminally underpriced: the field reruns its own past on a delay (MoE from 1991, LSTMs from 1997)
