# Knowledge-Graph-Learning

## Definition
An approach to learning that structures notes as a network of connected concept nodes rather than a linear collection. Every new concept is connected to existing concepts, making each subsequent concept more valuable because there are more existing nodes to connect to.

## Why It Matters
The connection is where understanding lives. A student who knows three separate concepts has surface familiarity. A student who understands how those three concepts relate to each other has understanding. The knowledge graph accelerates connection-building through Claude's ability to reason across the full vault simultaneously.

## Key Ideas
- The 10th concept is more valuable than the first because there are 9 existing concepts for it to connect to
- Every concept added must be connected to at least two existing concepts before being considered complete
- Claude finds connections that sequential reading cannot produce
- The graph stores non-sequentially even though you studied sequentially
- Individual flashcards test individual facts; the connection protocol tests relationships

## Tradeoffs
- Requires a minimum number of concepts before the graph produces value
- Connection quality depends on Claude's reasoning across the vault
