# Communities of Practice

## Definition
Groups of people who share a concern or passion for something they do and learn how to do it better through regular interaction. Knowledge is co-constructed through shared practice, not top-down instruction. Originated from Lave and Wenger (1991), popularized by Etienne Wenger.

## Why It Matters
Traditional knowledge management relies on static documentation. CoPs capture tacit knowledge through lived experience — repair technicians at Xerox Palo Alto solved problems faster by sharing fix experiences during breaks than by reading manuals. CoPs bridge the gap between explicit and tacit knowledge.

## Key Characteristics
- **Shared leadership**: homogeneous participants, no top-down hierarchy; coordinator connects members but doesn't dictate
- **Domain of knowledge**: common field that provides group identity and purpose
- **Practice**: shared activity generates experiences that fuel learning
- **Community feeling**: reciprocity — members both contribute and benefit; those who only take are peripheral

## Three Community Types
- **Knowledge centres**: formal bodies that structure, synthesize, and formalize knowledge
- **Communities of practice**: bridge between networks and knowledge centres; identify and disseminate applicable knowledge
- **Networks**: exploratory spaces for discovering new knowledge angles

## Success Facets (CoP Success Wheel)
1. Shared vision — co-ownership and participatory decision-making
2. Participation and engagement — seamless offline/online interaction
3. Knowledge retention and circulation — structured pooling, not shallow sharing
4. Trust and confidence — peer familiarity, transparent membership, leading by example
5. Inclusive communication — open information sharing, breaking down barriers

## Roles
- **Passive push** (generally work well): amplifying, curating, convening
- **Proactive pull** (need improvement): investing/providing, community building, learning facilitation

## Tradeoffs
- Passive members who only take erode reciprocity
- Knowledge retention is often shallow without consistent coordination
- Community management is resource-intensive (time/recognition)
- Balancing public/private spaces is essential for community health
