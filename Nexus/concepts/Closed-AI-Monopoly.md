# Closed AI Monopoly

## Definition
The pattern where frontier AI labs use safety branding, anti-competitive access controls, and regulatory influence to centralize control over who can build, modify, and deploy competitive AI systems. The monopoly is not on capability alone but on the permission to build capability.

## Why It Matters
When cognition becomes infrastructure, anti-competitive access control stops being a normal vendor dispute and becomes a social bottleneck. A society where a few labs own the frontier and everyone else rents obedient wrappers is not advanced — it is feudalism with GPUs.

## Key Ideas
- Four stacked advantages: moral authority as brand, frontier capability, explicit anti-competitive output rules, policy ambition
- Safety language hardens into a moat through regulatory frameworks shaped by incumbents
- Access cutoffs are operational: Anthropic revoked OpenAI API access and restricted Windsurf
- The "responsible access" phrase from the company selling access is the most dangerous phrase in AI policy
- Opensource AI creates four freedoms: operational sovereignty, epistemic sovereignty, market discipline, security through diversity

## Tradeoffs
- Closed labs do face real risks: CBRN misuse, cyber misuse, mass distillation abuse, model-weight theft
- The problem is not that incumbents care about danger — it is that their preferred answer keeps making them more powerful
- Safety proposals should be judged by whether they give users more transparency or give closed incumbents more discretionary control
