## Jana Saad

](/in/janasaad/)

Legal AI Researcher | EU AI Act & AI Compliance Consultant | AI Governance Consultant | Legal Trainer | Mediator | MENA’s 1st Legal Marketing Strategist | Legal AI Strategist

*Prepared with data up to 13 February 2026 (Asia/Beirut). Where the* ***Digital Omnibus*** *proposal is referenced, note that it is still being debated in the legislative process and has* ***not yet taken effect****.*

The European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) is the first comprehensive AI law globally. While much attention has focused on prohibited practices and high‑risk systems, **Article 4** introduces a novel principle: *AI literacy*. From 2 February 2025, all providers and deployers of AI systems must ensure that their staff—and “other persons” acting on their behalf—possess a **sufficient level of AI literacy**. Enforcement by national market‑surveillance authorities starts on 3 August 2026. This guide explains the components of AI literacy, identifies who must comply, outlines minimum training expectations, details documentation obligations, describes enforcement procedures, suggests proposed amendments, and provides resources for developing effective AI literacy programs.

### 1\. Article 4 of the AI Act: definitions and scope

### 1.1 What Article 4 requires

Article 4 states that both **providers** (those placing AI systems on the EU market) and **deployers** (entities using AI systems under their authority) “shall ensure, to the extent of their best effort,” that their staff and *other persons acting on their behalf* have **sufficient AI literacy**. The duty applies **universally across all AI systems,** not just high‑risk ones, and is **technology‑neutral**. Providers must consider individuals’ technical knowledge, experience, education, and the **context of use**. The aim is to enable informed decisions about deploying AI systems, awareness of benefits and risks, and proper human oversight.

### 1.2 What is “AI literacy”?

The Commission’s Q&A defines **AI literacy** as the **skills, knowledge, and understanding** needed to effectively and ethically deploy AI systems and to assess their impacts. It covers:

- **Technical understanding**: how AI works, its capabilities, limitations, and failure modes.
- **Legal and ethical awareness**: knowledge of applicable laws and ethical principles (e.g., non-discrimination, fundamental rights).
- **Context‑specific competence**: understanding of the organization’s role (provider vs. deployer), the risk level of AI systems used, and appropriate human oversight.
- **Critical thinking**: the ability to question outputs, recognize biases, and decide when human intervention is required.

AI literacy is not confined to technical staff; managers, project managers, sales teams, and end‑users must receive training appropriate to their role.

### 1.3 Who must be AI‑literate? Understanding the “other person’s” concept

The duty extends beyond employees. **“Other persons”** include **contractors, service providers, temporary agency workers, self‑employed persons, partners, and certain clients**. For example, a marketing agency using an AI‑powered advertisement generator on behalf of a client must ensure that its personnel understand the tool’s limitations and legal obligations. Similarly, organizations employing third‑party data‑labelers or AI developers must verify they are appropriately trained.

### 1.4 Extraterritorial reach

The AI Act applies to organizations **outside the EU** if their AI systems or outputs affect users in the EU. Article 2 states that providers and deployers established in third countries fall under the Act when their systems are placed on the EU market or their **output is used in the EU**. To enforce these extraterritorial obligations, non‑EU providers of high‑risk or general‑purpose AI models must appoint an **authorized representative in the EU**, responsible for maintaining documentation and cooperating with authorities. Recital 22 clarifies that certain AI systems are covered even if not placed on the market but used by EU‑based operators. Therefore, companies worldwide offering AI‑enabled services to EU clients must prepare for Article 4 obligations.

### 1.5 No official categories of AI systems for literacy purposes

Unlike the high‑risk catalogue in Annex III, Article 4 does **not** introduce formal categories of AI systems such as generative AI, conversational AI, or chatbots. AI literacy programs should be **risk-based,** focusing on the specific AI systems an organization uses and their impact on individuals rather than on labels. Organizations should classify systems into minimal‑risk, limited‑risk, high‑risk, or prohibited practices (per the Act) and provide training accordingly.

### 2\. Minimum content and risk‑based training

### 2.1 Core components

The Commission’s guidance lists four **minimum expectations** for AI literacy programs:

1. **General understanding of AI**—concepts, capabilities, and limitations.
2. **Role of the organization—whether** it acts as a provider, deployer, or both.
3. **Context of use and risk level**—what type of AI systems are used (minimal-, limited-, or high‑risk) and the potential impact on individuals.
4. **Tailoring to target groups**—adjusting depth and complexity depending on the audience’s knowledge and functions.

These elements reflect a **proportionality principle**. A developer working on a high‑risk medical device requires deeper technical and legal training than a marketing team using a low‑risk AI tool to draft social‑media posts.

### 2.2 Risk‑based approach

Article 4 emphasizes a **risk‑based approach**: training should be **more extensive for high‑risk AI systems** and minimal for low‑risk ones. Ropes & Gray summarizes that compliance depends on the **specific context**; there is no one‑size‑fits‑all solution. For high-risk areas such as jobs, education, or important infrastructure, understanding AI should cover the necessary Annex III rules, how to manage risks, handle data, and ensure human oversight. Conversely, low‑risk tools may only require basic awareness and human‑in‑the‑loop training.

### 2.3 Training formats and alternatives

The Q&A clarifies that **training is not strictly mandatory**; other initiatives such as workshops, mentoring, or self‑study can contribute to AI literacy. However, simply relying on user manuals or ad hoc instructions is **insufficient**. Organizations should combine multiple methods:

- **Formal training programmes** (online courses, classroom sessions)
- **Role‑specific workshops** (e.g., regulatory compliance for legal teams, technical deep dives for developers)
- **Learning by doing**—supervised practice with AI systems and case‑study simulations
- **Peer‑to‑peer sessions** and communities of practice

Law firms advise designing **modular programs** that differentiate between entry‑level awareness and advanced specialization. Training should be **continuous**, with periodic refreshers as AI systems evolve.

### 2.4 Scope for high‑risk industries

There are **no industry‑specific AI literacy mandates**, but sectors subject to high‑risk rules (healthcare, financial services, and education) must integrate relevant laws (e.g., medical device regulation and anti‑discrimination law) into training. For instance, a bank deploying a credit‑scoring AI must ensure staff understand algorithmic bias, fairness requirements, and obligations under the AI Act and financial regulation.

### 2.5 Measuring AI literacy and certification

There is **no obligation to formally test or certify employees**. Organizations may use internal assessments or certificates to track training, but they are optional. The Act does **not** require an **AI officer** or an AI governance board. However, many companies still establish AI governance committees to oversee compliance and coordinate training.

### 3 Compliance obligations and documentation

### 3.1 Timeline and transitional periods

The duty to ensure AI literacy **took effect on February 2, 2025**. Enforcement by national authorities will start on **3 August 2026**; until then, organizations should prepare by designing training programs and documenting their efforts. High‑risk obligations under Annex III enter into force on 2 August 2027 (with a one‑year transition).

### 3.2 Documentation and record‑keeping

While there is no requirement to obtain an external certificate, **organizations must keep internal records** of the measures they take to ensure AI literacy. Documentation should include:

- Inventory of AI systems used and their risk classification.
- Target groups (staff, contractors, and clients) and training modules assigned.
- Dates, duration, and content of training sessions or initiatives.
- Evidence of participant completion or attendance.
- Feedback and continuous‑improvement actions.

Proper documentation can demonstrate a “best effort” in case of audits or investigations. It also supports private litigation defense; employees or clients may sue if inadequate training causes harm, so records prove diligence.

### 3.3 Interactions with service providers and clients

Companies should update **contracts with service providers** to ensure that subcontractors using AI on their behalf meet AI literacy requirements. Similarly, clients using AI systems provided by the organization may need information or training. A simple clause could require the service provider to certify that its personnel have undergone AI literacy training tailored to the AI system’s risk level. This ensures that all persons handling AI outputs are knowledgeable and reduces liability.

### 4 Enforcement and penalties

### 4.1 Who enforces Article 4?

Enforcement lies primarily with **national market‑surveillance authorities**. They will assess whether organizations have made **best-effort** attempts to ensure AI literacy. The **AI Office** and the Commission may support with guidance but do not directly impose penalties.

### 4.2 Penalties and aggravating factors

Article 4 does **not** impose standalone fines. However, failure to ensure AI literacy may act as a **mitigating or aggravating factor** when authorities evaluate violations of other AI Act obligations. Penalties for high‑risk systems can reach up to **€15 million or 3% of global annual turnover**; for prohibited practices, up to **€35 million or 7%.** Lack of AI literacy could increase the severity of fines or lead to orders to halt operations. Private enforcement allows employees or individuals harmed by an AI system to sue organizations for negligence.

### 4.3 Extraterritorial enforcement

Given the AI Act’s extraterritorial reach, non‑EU providers serving EU users must appoint an **authorized representative** and comply with Article 4. Failing to do so could result in penalties, denial of market access, or orders to suspend services. Market‑surveillance authorities may collaborate across borders and rely on the EU’s authorized representative to enforce compliance.

### 5\. AI Office approach and guidance

### 5.1 Living repository and resources

The **AI Office** has developed a **living repository** to collect and share AI literacy practices. By mid‑2025 it included over **40 practices** from industry and public authorities. Replicating these practices does **not guarantee compliance**, but they provide inspiration for program design. The repository is complemented by webinars, AI Pact events, and Q&A updates. The AI Office invites organizations to submit their practices and will update the repository regularly.

### 5.2 Commission and AI Office plans

Within the Commission itself, the AI Office is developing **internal training packages** and a **community of practice**. The Commission encourages EU agencies and institutions to access these materials and adapt them to their specific contexts. Upcoming guidance will cover **human oversight, risk management, and high‑risk compliance**. These non‑binding guidelines will be issued before the enforcement date to assist providers and deployers.

### 5.3 No mandatory AI officer

The Q&A clarifies that companies do **not** need to appoint a dedicated AI officer; a **data protection officer (DPO)** may also perform AI compliance functions. Nonetheless, many organizations find it prudent to establish an **AI governance board** that oversees compliance, risk management, and training.

### 6 Proposed amendments: the Digital Omnibus

In November 2025 the Commission published a **Digital Omnibus** proposal to simplify digital regulation. Regarding AI literacy, the proposal **removes the direct obligation on providers and deployers** and instead tasks **Member States and the Commission** with **encouraging AI literacy through training and resources**. The European Data Protection Board and Supervisor criticized this shift, arguing that it weakens preventive safeguards. The proposal introduces a new Article 4a allowing certain sensitive data processing to detect bias and delays the start date for high‑risk obligations. As of February 2026, these amendments are still under negotiation. Therefore, organizations should continue to prepare for Article 4 as it is enacted while monitoring legislative updates.

### 7\. Resources, frameworks and support

### 7.1 Frameworks and competency models

The Commission recommends drawing on existing digital‑skills frameworks:

- **DigComp 2.2 – Digital Competence Framework for Citizens**: outlines competencies such as data literacy, communication, and problem-solving.
- **UNESCO’s AI competency frameworks** for **teachers** and **students** address ethical awareness, AI concepts, and pedagogy.
- **Ethical guidelines on AI and data in education** (developed by the Commission and OECD).

These can be adapted to different audiences, including parents, children, and non‑technical users.

### 7.2 Training resources and support for SMEs

Small and medium‑sized enterprises (SMEs) may lack resources to develop comprehensive training. The AI Office suggests:

- Leveraging the **European Digital Innovation Hubs (EDIHs)** network for workshops and hands‑on training.
- The Digital Skills and Jobs Platform provides free courses, best practices, and a community of experts.
- Participating in **AI Pact webinars** and AI Office events, which provide insights into compliance and training strategies.
- Accessing resources from industry associations and legal advisories (e.g., law‑firm toolkits and webinars).

### 7.3 Living repository access

The living repository is available through the Commission’s Digital Strategy portal. Organizations can read summaries and download full reports. Each entry describes the context, target group, training content, and lessons learned, enabling organizations to benchmark their programs.

### 8 Implementation roadmap: practical steps for organisations

To comply with Article 4, organizations can follow this **step‑by‑step roadmap**:

1. **Map AI systems** and classify them by risk (prohibited, high‑risk, limited‑risk, minimal‑risk). Maintain an inventory.
2. **Identify roles and target groups**: employees, contractors, external partners, and clients who interact with AI outputs.
3. **Assess baseline knowledge**: evaluate existing skills and identify gaps. While formal testing is not required, simple surveys or interviews help tailor training.
4. **Develop a risk‑based training program** covering general AI concepts, organization‑specific roles, and relevant laws. Use modular content with varying depth.
5. **Choose delivery formats**: combine e‑learning, workshops, mentoring, and on‑the‑job practice.
6. **Include ethical and legal considerations**: integrate GDPR, anti‑discrimination laws, sector‑specific rules, and human‑oversight obligations. Address common risks like bias, transparency, and explainability.
7. **Update contracts** with service providers to ensure their personnel meet AI literacy requirements.
8. **Document everything**: keep records of the program design, training sessions, participants, and continuous‑improvement actions.
9. **Regularly update training**: revise content as AI technologies and regulations evolve. Leverage the living repository and AI Office guidance.
10. **Monitor legislative developments**: follow the progress of the Digital Omnibus and other amendments to anticipate changes.

### 9 Frequently asked questions (FAQs)

**Question Answer**

**Does my company need to test employees’ knowledge or obtain a certificate?** No. Article 4 does not require formal testing or certification. Internal assessments and documentation are recommended for proof of effort.

**Is an AI officer or governance board mandatory?** No, but many organizations establish governance boards voluntarily; a DPO can cover AI compliance.

**Do clients or contractors need training?** Yes. The term “other persons” includes contractors and clients acting on the deployer’s behalf. Contracts should address this requirement.

**Are there sector‑specific literacy rules (e.g., for finance or healthcare)?** Not explicitly. All sectors must ensure AI literacy proportionate to the risk level and existing sectoral regulations.

**Are generative AI and chatbots treated differently?** No. The Act does not classify AI systems by type for literacy purposes; it focuses on risk level and context.

**How will the law be enforced?** National market‑surveillance authorities will evaluate compliance and may consider lack of AI literacy as an aggravating factor when assessing other violations.

**When must we be ready?** The obligation applies since 2 Feb 2025; enforcement starts 3 Aug 2026. Start preparing now to demonstrate best efforts.

**10\. Conclusion**

Article 4 of the EU AI Act positions **AI literacy** at the heart of responsible AI governance. By requiring providers and deployers to ensure that staff and collaborators understand AI’s technical and legal dimensions, the regulation aims to mitigate risks and foster trust. The duty is **universal** and **extraterritorial**; it applies to all AI systems and to companies worldwide serving EU users. Although training and certification are flexible, organizations must adopt a **risk‑based, documented, and continuous approach**. While the Digital Omnibus may alter these obligations, prudent organizations will build robust AI literacy programs now to safeguard their operations, enhance employee skills, and demonstrate accountability.
