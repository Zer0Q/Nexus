# AI Literacy and Article 4

## Summary
Article 4 of the EU AI Act turns AI literacy into an organizational obligation for providers and deployers of AI systems. The guide emphasizes that literacy is risk-based, role-specific, documented, and tied to actual system use rather than a generic training checkbox.

## Core Concepts
- [[concepts/AI-Literacy]] -- the practical capability to understand AI systems, risks, limitations, and responsible use in context.
- [[concepts/EU-AI-Act-Article-4]] -- the AI Act obligation requiring providers and deployers to ensure sufficient AI literacy among relevant people.
- [[tools/AI-Governance]] -- governance practices translate legal obligations into roles, training records, controls, and monitoring.
- [[concepts/Risk-by-Design]] -- literacy depth should scale with the risk, sector, user role, and system impact.
- [[concepts/Trustworthiness]] -- AI literacy supports informed oversight, appropriate reliance, and safer deployment.

## Key Insights
- Article 4 applies broadly to organizations using or providing AI, not only to high-risk AI providers.
- The guide rejects a one-size-fits-all curriculum: training should match the AI system, user role, level of autonomy, and exposure to risk.
- Documentation matters because organizations need evidence of training scope, attendance, content, and periodic updates.
- Service providers and clients create shared literacy dependencies when one party operates or supports AI systems for another.
- AI Office guidance and repositories are useful, but organizations still need their own implementation roadmap and record-keeping.
