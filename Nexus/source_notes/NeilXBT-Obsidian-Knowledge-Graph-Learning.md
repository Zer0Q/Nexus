# Obsidian Knowledge Graph for Accelerated Learning

## Summary
Passive study methods (re-reading, highlighting) create an illusion of knowledge. This article presents a five-module learning system built on [[Active-Recall-Learning]] and [[Spaced-Repetition-System]], implemented through Obsidian concept notes connected by Claude-driven [[Connection-Protocol]]. The knowledge graph transforms studying from passive review into systematic retrieval practice, with [[Synthesis-Session]] producing integrated understanding documents that prove comprehension.

## Core Concepts
- [[Active-Recall-Learning]] -- deliberate retrieval from memory vs passive review
- [[Spaced-Repetition-System]] -- reviewing at Ebbinghaus forgetting curve intervals
- [[Knowledge-Graph-Learning]] -- learning through connected concept nodes
- [[Concept-Note-Template]] -- structured format for learning notes with testability
- [[Connection-Protocol]] -- finding non-obvious connections after study sessions
- [[Mastery-Score-Tracking]] -- frontmatter mastery scoring driving review intervals
- [[Synthesis-Session]] -- bi-weekly integrated understanding documents
- [[Pattern-Identification-Across-Notes]] -- emergent patterns across concept notes
- [[Predictive-Test-Method]] -- novel scenario prediction to test deep understanding
- [[Gap-Analysis-Study]] -- identifying missing concepts that would most increase understanding
- [[Sequential-vs-Network-Learning]] -- linear study vs graph-based connected study

## Key Insights
- Familiarity is not the same as being able to use knowledge
- The discomfort of not being able to remember is evidence that learning is happening
- Active recall + spaced repetition improves exam performance by 15-20%
- The connection is where understanding lives, not in isolated nodes
- Every concept added must connect to at least two existing concepts before being complete
- The 10th concept is more valuable than the first because there are 9 existing concepts to connect to
- Writing a concept in your own words is the first act of retrieval practice
- Synthesis documents prove understanding rather than familiarity
